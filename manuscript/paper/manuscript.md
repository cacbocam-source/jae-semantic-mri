Methods Overview

This study employed a computational text analysis pipeline to examine the historical evolution of disciplinary knowledge within the Journal of Agricultural Education from 1960 to 2026. The pipeline extracts, cleans, segments, and quantitatively analyzes manuscript text using reproducible processing procedures designed to accommodate both digitally-native and archival documents.

Manuscripts were ingested from a curated corpus of journal articles stored on a dedicated research storage volume. Because the historical corpus contains both modern digital PDFs and older scanned documents, a dual-route extraction architecture was implemented. Digitally-native articles were processed using direct text extraction, while legacy manuscripts were recovered using optical character recognition (OCR). Extracted text was normalized and truncated at bibliographic boundaries (e.g., "References," "Literature Cited," or "Acknowledgements") to remove citation noise and preserve only manuscript content.

Following extraction and cleaning, manuscripts were segmented into conceptual sections representing theoretical framing, introduction, methods, and results. Because section conventions vary across decades of publication, the pipeline employed an era-aware segmentation strategy combining proximity-based heuristics for early manuscripts with header detection for modern articles. The resulting section text provides standardized analytical units across the full historical corpus.

For analytical modeling, selected manuscript sections will be transformed into semantic vector representations using a transformer-based embedding model. Manuscripts will be grouped into five-year historical epochs, and epoch-level centroids will be computed to represent the semantic center of disciplinary discourse within each period. Two quantitative metrics will then be calculated: semantic dispersion, measuring conceptual diversity within epochs, and innovation velocity, measuring the rate of conceptual change between successive epochs. Statistical testing will evaluate differences in dispersion across historical eras and identify potential change points in the temporal evolution of the discipline.

To ensure reproducibility, all documents are tracked through a corpus ledger recording extraction routes, processing metadata, and output artifacts. Automated environment diagnostics and audit logging verify the computational environment and pipeline integrity throughout the analysis.
